Why Requirements Matter in BI Projects

  • Building the wrong dashboard wastes time, money, and trust
  • SMEs have limited resources - must get it right the first time
  • Poor requirements = dashboards that nobody uses
  • Good requirements = actionable insights that drive decisions
  • Understanding needs upfront prevents costly rework

Case Study - The Cost of Poor Requirements

Scenario: Local Retail Chain Example

Problem: Built a complex inventory dashboard with 50+ metrics

Issue: Store managers only needed 3 key numbers:

  • Stock levels
  • Reorder points
  • Turnover rate

Result: Dashboard abandoned after 2 weeks. KES 300,000 wasted.

Lesson: More data ≠ Better decisions. Know what users actually need.

The BI Requirements Challenge

  • Stakeholders often don't know what they need
  • They ask for "everything" or "just give me a dashboard"
  • Technical teams build what's asked, not what's needed
  • Result: Beautiful dashboards with no business impact
  • YOUR JOB: Bridge the gap between business needs and technical solutions

Stakeholder Analysis for SMEs

Who needs what information?

  • Business Owner/CEO: Strategic overview, profitability, growth trends
  • Department Managers: Operational metrics, team performance, departmental KPIs
  • End Users: Daily operational data, task-specific metrics, real-time alerts
  • Each level needs different detail and frequency

Information Needs by Role

Executive Level:
  • High-level KPIs
  • Trends over time
  • Monthly/quarterly views
  • Strategic decisions
Operational Level:
  • Detailed transactions
  • Real-time/daily data
  • Actionable alerts
  • Tactical decisions

Requirements Gathering Techniques

Four main approaches:

  1. Interviews: One-on-one conversations with stakeholders
  2. Observation: Watch how they currently work with data
  3. Document Analysis: Review existing reports and spreadsheets
  4. Workshops: Collaborative sessions with multiple stakeholders

Each technique reveals different insights!

Effective Interview Questions

What to ask SME stakeholders:

  • "What decisions do you make daily/weekly/monthly?"
  • "What information do you wish you had right now?"
  • "How do you currently track [X metric]?"
  • "What keeps you up at night about the business?"
  • "If you could see one number every morning, what would it be?"
  • "What reports do you look at most often?"

The Power of "Why?"

Example conversation:

Stakeholder: "I need to see all customer transactions"

You: "Why is that important?"

Stakeholder: "To identify our best customers"

You: "Why do you need to identify best customers?"

Stakeholder: "To focus retention efforts on high-value customers"

REAL NEED: Customer segmentation by value, not transaction lists!

Always dig deeper with "why" questions.

Observation Technique

Learning by watching:

  • Watch stakeholders work with current reports
  • What do they look at first?
  • What do they ignore or skip?
  • What calculations do they do manually?
  • What frustrates them about current processes?
  • Where do they spend most time?

Remember: Actions speak louder than words!

Document Analysis

Analyzing existing reports and spreadsheets:

  • Collect existing reports and Excel files
  • What metrics are already being tracked?
  • How is data currently formatted?
  • What formulas are being used?
  • What gets printed or emailed regularly?

These documents show what users actually value - build on what works, improve what doesn't.

PART 2: From Business Questions to KPIs

Duration: 35-40 minutes

Start with Business Questions

What decisions need to be made?

Common SME business questions:

  • "Which products should we stock more of?"
  • "Which customers should we focus retention efforts on?"
  • "What times/days should we staff more heavily?"
  • "Are our promotions working?"
  • "Is the business growing or shrinking?"
  • "Where are we losing money?"

The KPI Development Framework

Four-step process:

  1. Business Question → What needs to be decided?
  2. Metric → What can be measured?
  3. KPI → What indicates success/failure?
  4. Visualization → How should it be displayed?

Always start with the business question, never with available data!

Example - Growth Question

Business Question: Is the business growing?

Metric: Monthly Revenue

KPI: Month-over-Month Revenue Growth %

Formula: ((Current Month - Previous Month) / Previous Month) × 100

Visualization: Line chart with trend line

Target: >10% monthly growth

Example - Product Performance

Business Question: Which products should we stock more of?

Metric: Product Sales Volume & Revenue

KPI: Product Revenue Contribution %

Formula: (Product Revenue / Total Revenue) × 100

Visualization: Bar chart ranked by contribution

Target: Top 20% products = 80% revenue (Pareto Principle)

Example - Customer Retention

Business Question: Are we keeping our customers?

Metric: Repeat Purchase Rate

KPI: Customer Retention Rate

Formula: (Customers who returned / Total customers) × 100

Visualization: Trend line over time with monthly points

Target: >60% retention rate

SMART KPI Criteria

Every KPI should be:

  • Specific: Clearly defined, no ambiguity
  • Measurable: Can be quantified with available data
  • Achievable: Realistic targets based on business context
  • Relevant: Tied directly to business goals
  • Time-bound: Specified time period (daily, weekly, monthly, yearly)

If it's not SMART, it's not a good KPI!

SMART vs. Vague KPIs

❌ Vague KPI: "Sales Performance"

  • What aspect of sales?
  • Compared to what?
  • What timeframe?
  • How is it measured?

✓ SMART KPI: "Month-over-Month Sales Growth %"

  • Specific metric (growth rate)
  • Clear comparison (current vs previous month)
  • Defined period (monthly)
  • Measurable formula

KPI Categories for SMEs

PART 3: KPI Documentation & Success Metrics

Duration: 20-25 minutes

KPI Documentation Template

Example: Month-over-Month Sales Growth

  • KPI Name: Month-over-Month Sales Growth
  • Definition: Percentage change in total sales from previous month
  • Formula: ((Current Month Sales - Previous Month Sales) / Previous Month Sales) × 100
  • Data Source: Sales transaction table
  • Owner: Sales Manager
  • Target: >10% growth
  • Update Frequency: Updated monthly, reviewed in monthly meetings
  • Why It Matters: Indicates business trajectory and helps identify seasonal patterns

Why Document KPIs?

Six critical reasons:

  • Ensures everyone understands the metric the same way
  • Makes calculations transparent and auditable
  • Helps new users understand the dashboard
  • Prevents "metric confusion" across departments
  • Essential for your project documentation!
  • Professional standard in BI projects

Good documentation = Better adoption

Dashboard Planning Principles

Key design principles:

  • The "One Big Number" principle: What's the MOST important metric?
  • KPI Hierarchy: Primary metrics → Supporting metrics
  • Supporting metrics explain WHY the primary changed
  • How many KPIs? Follow the 7±2 rule (5-9 maximum per page)
  • Too many metrics = decision paralysis
  • Group related KPIs together

Dashboard KPI Hierarchy Example

PRIMARY KPI (Top of dashboard):

  • Monthly Revenue: KES 2.5M

SECONDARY KPIs (Support the primary):

  • MoM Growth: +15%
  • Avg Transaction Value: KES 850
  • Total Transactions: 2,941

TERTIARY KPIs (Explain the details):

  • Top Product Category: Laptops (30%)
  • Top Location: Westlands Branch
  • Peak Sales Day: Saturday

Aligning KPIs with BI Maturity

Match your KPIs to organizational maturity:

  • Level 1-2 (Descriptive): "What happened?"
    • Basic metrics: totals, counts, averages
  • Level 3-4 (Diagnostic): "Why did it happen?"
    • Comparative analysis, trends, correlations
  • Level 5 (Predictive): "What will happen?"
    • Forecasting, predictive analytics

For your project: Likely Level 2-3 (descriptive to diagnostic)

Maturity Level Examples

Descriptive (Level 1-2):
  • Total sales this month: KES 500,000
  • Number of transactions: 450
  • Top 10 products by revenue
  • Total customer count: 1,200
Diagnostic (Level 3-4):
  • Sales vs. last month: +15%
  • Product performance trend over 6 months
  • Customer retention rate: 68%
  • Seasonality patterns identified
